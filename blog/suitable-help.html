“I Will Make You a Suitable Helper” (Gen 2:18): Learning to Wait, Learning to Choose

Reflections on marriage, discernment, and God's plan

Home › Blog › I Will Make You a Suitable Helper
Marriage & Family · Spiritual Reflections · January 29, 2026 · By Doris Perez
“The Lord God said: It is not good for the man to be alone; I will make him a helper suitable for him.” Genesis 2:18

This verse is perhaps one of the most quoted yet least meditated upon passages when we speak of marriage. We hear it at weddings, in preparation courses, in homilies... but do we truly understand what God meant when He spoke these words? And more importantly: do we know what He asks of us?

Because this verse is not simply about companionship. It speaks of a divine design. God looks at the human person and sees something incomplete — not because the person is broken, but because they were created for communion. And from that gaze of love, He decides to act: “I will make him a helper suitable for him.”

But here comes the question many avoid: What if the person you chose is not the one God prepared for you? What if, out of haste, fear, or loneliness, you took a path that was never His plan?

The great mistake: not knowing how to wait

I have seen it many times throughout years of accompaniment: people who choose a partner not from interior freedom, but from the fear of being alone. People who rush into a relationship because everyone around them is getting married. People who confuse attraction with vocation, emotion with discernment.

Not knowing how to wait is one of the greatest errors in vocational life. And I am not speaking only of passive waiting. I am speaking of an active waiting — one that prays, that seeks formation, that heals wounds, that grows in self-knowledge.

When you do not know yourself, you cannot recognize who truly complements you.

When you have not healed your wounds, you tend to look for someone who fills your voids rather than someone who walks beside you. And when you have not learned to listen to God in silence, you fill that silence with noise — with the first voice that says “I love you.”

God does not ask us to wait forever. He asks us to wait well. To wait in His presence. To let Him work while we prepare our hearts. Because a heart that has not prepared itself cannot receive the gift God wants to give it.

Waiting is not emptiness. It is sacred time. It is the workshop where God shapes your heart so it can love as He loves: with freedom, with truth, with total self-giving.

What does “suitable helper” really mean?

The Hebrew expression ezer kenegdo is far richer than our translations sometimes convey. Ezer means helper, but it is the same word used in the Psalms to describe God Himself as our help (Psalm 121:2). It is not a subordinate help, but a powerful, vital, necessary help.

And kenegdo means “opposite to him” — as in a mirror, as a counterpart. Someone who stands face to face, who corresponds, who complements. Not someone identical, but someone who brings what the other lacks.

A “suitable helper” is not someone who simply makes your life easier or agrees with everything you say. It is someone who challenges you to holiness. Someone who helps you become the person God created you to be. Someone who, with their gifts and even with their limitations, draws you closer to God.

True complementarity is not measured by personality tests or shared hobbies. It is measured by a shared mission: to help each other get to Heaven.

This is why choosing poorly has such profound consequences. If the person by your side does not help you grow in faith, in virtue, in truth — no matter how much love there appears to be — something fundamental is missing. Marriage was not designed as a reward for attraction, but as a path of mutual sanctification.

Three keys to discerning the suitable helper

  1. Know yourself in God

    Before asking “Who is the right person for me?”, you need to ask: “Who am I in God's eyes?” Self-knowledge is the foundation of every good discernment. You need to know your wounds, your gifts, your attachments, your fears. You need to have walked a path of interior healing and spiritual growth. Because only from wholeness can you give yourself freely — and only from freedom can you receive the other as a gift.

  2. Observe the fruits

    Jesus said: “By their fruits you will know them” (Mt 7:16). This is perhaps the most practical key to discernment. Does this relationship bear fruits of peace, of joy, of growth? Or does it bring anxiety, confusion, and distance from God? Do you pray more or less since this person entered your life? Are you closer to the sacraments or farther away? The fruits do not lie.

  3. Seek spiritual accompaniment

    No one should discern marriage alone. A spiritual director, a wise priest, an experienced married couple — someone who can see what you sometimes cannot. Someone who loves you enough to tell you the truth. Discernment is not a private matter; it is ecclesial. The Church walks with you, and God speaks through those He places on your path.

Trusting in God's timing

God's promise in Genesis 2:18 is not merely a theological statement. It is an invitation to trust. To trust that He who created you also created someone for you — and that His timing, though it may test your patience, is always perfect.

If you are in a season of waiting, do not waste it. Use it to grow, to heal, to deepen your relationship with God. And if you are already in a relationship, bring these questions to prayer: Is this person helping me become a saint? Am I helping them?

The “suitable helper” is not a fairy tale or a fantasy. It is God's plan for those who are called to marriage. And that plan is worth waiting for, worth praying for, and worth choosing with the full freedom of a heart surrendered to God.

Lord, give us the courage to wait for Your plan, the wisdom to recognize the one You have prepared for us, and the freedom to choose from love and not from fear. Amen.
